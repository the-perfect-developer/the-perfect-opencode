## Finding Seed Keywords

Start broad, then narrow down.

### Brainstorming Sources
- Core products and services the business offers
- Problems the target audience is trying to solve
- Questions customers ask in sales calls or support tickets
- Competitor site navigation and blog categories
- Reddit, Quora, and niche forums for natural language patterns
- Google's "People Also Ask" and "Related Searches" sections

### Expanding Seeds
Enter seed keywords into a keyword tool and explore:
- **Phrase match** — Keywords containing the seed phrase
- **Questions** — Who, what, when, where, why, how variations
- **Related terms** — Semantically related keywords

## Evaluating Keywords

Assess each keyword on four dimensions:

### 1. Search Volume
Monthly searches for the keyword. Higher volume = more potential traffic. Note that volume estimates vary between tools; treat them as directional, not precise.

### 2. Keyword Difficulty (KD)
A 0–100 score estimating how hard it is to rank on page one. Compare KD to the site's domain authority:
- New sites (DA < 20): Target KD 0–20
- Mid-authority sites (DA 20–50): Target KD 20–40
- Established sites (DA 50+): Can compete for KD 40+

### 3. Traffic Potential
The total organic traffic the top-ranking page receives for that keyword and all its variants. Traffic potential is often a better metric than raw search volume because it accounts for related queries a single page can rank for.

### 4. Business Relevance
Can the site monetize or convert traffic from this keyword? A high-volume keyword with no path to conversion is not worth targeting.

## Search Intent Analysis

Search intent is the underlying goal behind a query. Google's algorithm is highly tuned to match content type to intent.

### The Four Intent Types

**Informational** — The searcher wants to learn.
- Signals: "how to," "what is," "why does," "guide," "tutorial"
- Content format: Blog post, guide, explainer, video

**Commercial Investigation** — The searcher is comparing options before buying.
- Signals: "best," "top," "vs," "review," "alternatives"
- Content format: Comparison article, listicle, review

**Transactional** — The searcher wants to complete an action (buy, sign up, download).
- Signals: "buy," "price," "discount," "free trial," "download"
- Content format: Product page, service page, landing page

**Navigational** — The searcher wants a specific website.
- Signals: Brand name + keyword, "login," "official site"
- Content format: Homepage, brand page

### How to Determine Intent

1. Search the keyword in Google
2. Examine the top 5 results: What content type dominates? (blog posts, product pages, videos?)
3. Note the content angle: Are results focused on "best of" lists, step-by-step guides, or product specs?
4. Match your content type and angle to what Google is already rewarding

Mismatching intent is one of the most common reasons well-optimized pages fail to rank.

## Long-Tail vs. Short-Tail Strategy

### Short-Tail Keywords
- 1–2 words: "SEO," "web design," "coffee"
- High volume, high competition
- Broad intent — harder to match precisely
- Appropriate for high-authority sites targeting brand awareness

### Long-Tail Keywords
- 3+ words: "how to do keyword research for a new website"
- Lower volume, lower competition
- Specific intent — easier to match and convert
- Appropriate for new sites and niche targeting

### Recommended Approach
Build a content cluster around a short-tail pillar topic, supported by long-tail cluster pages. The pillar page targets the broad keyword; cluster pages capture long-tail variants and funnel authority back to the pillar.

## Keyword Cannibalization

Keyword cannibalization occurs when multiple pages on the same site compete for the same keyword. This splits ranking signals and confuses search engines about which page to surface.

### Identifying Cannibalization
Search `site:yourdomain.com "target keyword"` in Google. If multiple pages appear, cannibalization may be occurring.

### Fixing Cannibalization
- **Merge** — Combine thin pages into one comprehensive page with a 301 redirect from the merged URL
- **Differentiate** — Adjust the focus of each page to target distinct keywords or intents
- **Canonicalize** — Set a canonical tag pointing to the preferred page if merging is not feasible
- **Noindex** — Remove low-value duplicate pages from the index

## Semantic SEO

Search engines use natural language processing to understand the relationships between terms. Optimizing for semantic relevance means covering a topic comprehensively, not just repeating the exact keyword.

### Semantic Optimization Techniques

**LSI (Latent Semantic Indexing) Keywords**
Related terms and synonyms that signal topical depth. Example: A page about "coffee brewing" should also mention "extraction," "grind size," "water temperature," and "pour over" — not just repeat "coffee brewing."

**Entity Optimization**
Named entities (people, places, organizations, concepts) help search engines understand context. Reference relevant entities naturally within content.

**Topic Coverage**
Analyze what subtopics top-ranking pages cover. Use tools like Ahrefs Content Gap or Semrush's SEO Writing Assistant to identify missing subtopics and add them.

**Natural Language**
Write in natural, conversational language. Google's algorithms are trained on human text; content that sounds robotic or keyword-stuffed performs worse than content that reads naturally.
